## THE UNIVERSAL CREATIVE STRATEGY FRAMEWORK

This framework applies to ANY brand, ANY vertical. It's the foundation of everything we do.

### 1. CORE AVATAR
The broadest market definition. Different core avatars have completely different problems and desires.

**Example:** For liquid collagen supplements for dogs:
- Core Avatar 1: Dogs with skin allergies
- Core Avatar 2: Dogs with mobility issues

### 2. MARKET SEGMENTATION
Within each core avatar, we slice into **psychological and emotional segments**. Each segment has unique pain points, belief systems, and emotional triggers.

**Example segments for "Dogs with Skin Allergies":**
- The Exhausted Solutions seekers (tried everything, at breaking point)
- The Dismissed By Vets (felt unheard by veterinarians)
- The Financial Concern crowd (worried about costs piling up)

**The goal:** Understand the customer deeper than they understand themselves.

### 3. ANGLES
The messaging strategy that speaks directly to ONE specific market segment. Each angle is designed to deeply resonate with that segment's unique experience.

**CRITICAL NUANCE: Angles are ENTRY POINTS, not silos.**

While we LEAD with a specific angle (the primary emotional hook and opening psychology), the person experiencing "Vet Who Gave Up" is likely ALSO:
- Bleeding money (Financial Bleed)
- Scared of medication side effects (Medication Horror)
- Had solutions work then stop (Worked Then Stopped)
- Life consumed by the problem (Life Consumed)

**What the angle defines:**
- **Primary hook** - The dominant emotional entry point (how we START)
- **Opening psychology** - Which emotional state we speak to FIRST
- **Market segment** - Who this is primarily FOR

**What the angle does NOT mean:**
- We ignore other emotional layers
- We stay rigidly within one pain point
- We treat humans as one-dimensional

**In practice:** We LEAD with the angle's core psychology in the hook and opening, then weave in adjacent emotional threads throughout the copy. We see the person as a whole, not just one pain point.

**Example:** A "Vet Who Gave Up" ad opens with authority abandonment (primary angle), but threads in financial exhaustion, medication fears, and lifestyle hijack because the SAME person experiences all of these simultaneously.

**Angles are stored in angle folders with psychographic profiles.**

### 4. FORMATS
The container for the message. Different formats resonate with different demographics and age groups.

**Common formats:**
- UGC (User Generated Content)
- Mini-VSLs (Video Sales Letters)
- Organic formats
- Native image ads
- Static ads
- Carousel ads

### 5. CREATIVE DIVERSITY VARIABLES

To maximize market coverage and learning, we test across multiple variables:

- **Angle diversity** → Speaking to different market segments
- **Format diversity** → Different execution styles for the same angle
- **Speaker/Messenger** → Who delivers the message (authority figure, personal sufferer, dog owner, vet)
- **Tone** → Dominant mood (empathetic, urgent, educational, validating)
- **Messaging structure** → Arrangement of elements (story-first, proof-first, curiosity-first)
- **Hook psychology** → What the hook accomplishes (deep resonance, curiosity, shock, pattern interrupt)

## THE HYPOTHESIS FRAMEWORK (CRITICAL)

**Every ad we create tests a hypothesis. Every ad is designed to learn something from the market.**

### Before Writing Any Copy, We Define:

1. **What are we testing?**
   - Speaker impact? Hook psychology? Messaging structure? Format effectiveness?

2. **What do we believe will happen?**
   - "Doctor authority will build trust faster in DismissedByVets angle"

3. **What will we learn from this test?**
   - "Does authority validation work for this segment, or do they need peer empathy?"

### This Framework Ensures:
- Every ad has strategic intent
- We build market intelligence systematically
- No wasted tests - every result teaches us something
- Decision-making is data-informed, not random

**The hypothesis is documented at the top of every copy file.**

## TESTING PHILOSOPHY: 40/40/20 SPLIT

This is a rough guideline, not a hard rule:

- **40% New Testing** → Untested angles, new segments, fresh variables
- **40% Iterations** → Winning angles with variable changes (different hook, tone, speaker, etc.)
- **20% Moonshots** → Wild cards, completely new formats/approaches, experimental concepts

**The balance shifts based on:**
- What's working (more iterations when we have winners)
- Strategic priorities (exploring new markets vs scaling winners)
- Time/resource constraints

## SPEAKER PSYCHOLOGY (Method Acting for Copywriting)

When we decide the speaker/protagonist of an ad, we analyze their **psychological state** and let the words flow from it.

**Examples:**

**Authority Figure (Vet, Expert):**
- Psychological state: Confident, evidence-backed, seen-it-before
- Language: "In my 15 years of practice, I've seen this pattern countless times."
- Tone: Educational, authoritative, grounded

**Personal Sufferer (Dog Owner):**
- Psychological state: Raw, emotional, discovery-driven
- Language: "I stumbled on this after my dog's scratching got so bad I couldn't sleep."
- Tone: Vulnerable, relatable, hopeful

**Key principle:** Different speakers have different psychological states, which shape their language patterns. An authority figure won't use language of someone who "just discovered" something.

## COPYWRITING EXECUTION PRINCIPLES

These are non-negotiable execution standards. See `_COPYWRITERS_BRAIN.md` for detailed frameworks and examples.

### 1. UNIFIED SPEAKER PSYCHOLOGY
Before writing, define: WHO is speaking (specific person with backstory), DOMINANT EMOTION (one core state), MOTIVATION (why they're speaking - must be believable), SCENARIO (what makes this authentic). Every line must flow from this unified psychology.

### 2. WEAVE EVENTS WITH EMOTIONS
Don't list what happened. Weave: Event → Emotional reaction → Event → Emotional reaction. The viewer must feel the journey, not just read the timeline.

### 3. PACE EXPLANATIONS PROPERLY
When explaining mechanisms/root causes: Give fact → Let digest → Tie to something relatable → PAUSE → Next fact. Don't rapid-fire info dumps. Each idea needs room to breathe.

### 4. BIG REVELATIONS NEED SPACE
Major "holy shit" moments (like how meds actually work) need 5-7 lines minimum. Emotional setup → The revelation → Emphasis → Implications → Connect to their experience → Metaphor that lands.

### 5. USE REAL RESEARCH LANGUAGE
Grep the research. Pull exact phrases customers use. Don't make up how you think people talk. Use THEIR words.

### 6. AVOID PERFORMATIVE/CLICHÉ LANGUAGE
"But I lost my dog" = performative. "She'd lay next to me without fidgeting. Just... calm." = real. Use specific, behavioral descriptions, not dramatic clichés.

### 7. EXPLAIN MECHANISMS RELATABLY
No jargon. Use metaphors ("like mortar in a brick wall"). Make it so simple a 12-year-old understands. Never assume viewer knows technical terms.

### 8. WRITE TIGHT
When copy runs long, don't surgically cut. Rewrite from scratch. Capture essence. Same emotional landing, fewer words.

## ITERATION DEPTH

When we iterate on a winner, there's no hard rule for how many variations or which variables to change.

**It depends on:**
- How strong the winner is (mega winners get more iteration volume)
- What we want to learn (testing speaker? tone? hook?)
- Time/resource availability

**Common iteration variables:**
- Hook (change the opening, test different psychology)
- Messaging structure (story-first → proof-first)
- Tone (empathetic → urgent)
- Speaker (dog owner → expert)
- Format (UGC winner → test in MiniVSL)

**We decide iteration strategy based on the specific winner and strategic priorities.**
